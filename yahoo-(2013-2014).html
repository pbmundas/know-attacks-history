Attack Timeline

Date/Time Action Actor/Details Evidence/Impact
August 2013 Initial breach (2013): Attackers exploit a vulnerability, possibly in Yahoo’s proprietary cookie generation code. Unknown hackers (suspected non-state actors) Compromised all 3 billion user accounts; stole names, emails, phone numbers, birth dates, MD5-hashed passwords, security questions.
August-September 2013 Data collection and exfiltration (2013): Stolen data and forged cookies enable password-less access. Attackers 32 million accounts accessed via cookies (2015-2016); data sold on darknet.
Early 2014 Initial breach (2014): Spear-phishing email compromises Yahoo employee credentials. Russian actors (Alexey Belan, FSB officers, Karim Baratov) Access to User Account Database; state-sponsored motives.
November-December 2014 Data exfiltration (2014): Copied 500 million account records to external servers. Alexey Belan Included names, emails, phone numbers, birth dates, bcrypt-hashed passwords, security questions.
October 2014-November 2016 Exploitation: Hackers use stolen data to target emails, gift codes, and other platforms (e.g., Gmail). Belan, Baratov, FSB officers Enabled espionage, phishing, and account takeovers.
July 2016 Yahoo investigates separate incident, uncovering 2014 breach scope. Yahoo Realized 500 million accounts affected; aware since 2014 but delayed disclosure.
September 22, 2016 Public disclosure (2014 breach): Yahoo announces 500 million accounts compromised. Yahoo Stock drops 3% ($1.3B loss); Verizon deal impacted.
December 14, 2016 Public disclosure (2013 breach): Yahoo reports 1 billion accounts affected. Yahoo Forced password resets, invalidated security questions.
October 3, 2017 Revised disclosure (2013 breach): All 3 billion accounts affected. Yahoo $117.5M settlement; Verizon deal cut by $350M.
April 2019 Legal resolution: $117.5M settlement for ~896 million accounts (194 million users). Yahoo/Verizon Covered damages, credit monitoring; SEC fine of $35M.

Dwell Time Analysis

Dwell time for the 2013 breach was approximately 3 years (August 2013 to July 2016, when Yahoo began investigating), and for the 2014 breach, about 2 years (early 2014 to July 2016), due to undetected access and delayed investigation.

Step 1: Initial Access (2013: Aug; 2014: Early 2014) Step 2: Collection (2013: Aug-Sep; 2014: Nov-Dec) Step 3: Exfiltration (2013: Aug-Sep; 2014: Nov-Dec) Step 4: Detection (July 2016)
2013: Exploited system vulnerability (cookie code). 2014: Spear-phishing compromised employee.
What went wrong: Weak encryption (MD5), no 2FA.		 Queried User Account Database for 3B (2013) and 500M (2014) records.
Factor: No anomaly detection for mass queries.		 Exfiltrated data to external servers; 2013 used forged cookies.
Factor: No DLP or egress filtering.		 Investigation triggered by separate incident; 2014 breach known since 2014 but not fully scoped.
Total time: Enabled massive data theft, espionage.

Dwell Time Insight: The dwell time was like a hidden termite infestation, eroding data over years due to inadequate monitoring and encryption. Experts: Median dwell time in 2013 was ~150 days (Mandiant); Yahoo’s multi-year dwell was extreme, amplified by weak security practices and delayed response.

Other causes: No DLP, no SIEM, ignored 2014 breach signals.
