Attack Timeline

Date/Time Action Actor/Details Evidence/Impact
October 2016 Initial access: Hackers use stolen GitHub credentials to access private repository containing AWS S3 access key. Brandon Charles Glover, Vasile Mereacre (teen hackers) Credentials obtained from dark web; key granted access to unencrypted S3 bucket.
October 24-28, 2016 Data collection: Hackers access S3 bucket, download 57 million user records and 600,000 driver license numbers. Glover, Mereacre Unencrypted data included names, emails, phone numbers; no credit card data affected.
Late October 2016 Ransom demand: Hackers contact Uber’s Chief Security Officer Joseph Sullivan, demanding $100,000 Bitcoin payment. Glover, Mereacre Threat to release data; Sullivan negotiates via encrypted chat.
November 2016 Ransom payment and cover-up: Uber pays $100,000 in Bitcoin, disguised as bug bounty; hackers sign NDAs. Uber (Sullivan) Payment made; hackers claim no data retained; breach concealed from FTC. |
November 2016–November 2017 Cover-up continues: Uber conceals breach during FTC investigation into 2014 breach. Uber (Sullivan) No public disclosure; internal investigation suppressed. |
November 21, 2017 Public disclosure: Uber announces breach affecting 57 million users and drivers. Uber Password resets, notifications; stock drops 3%. |
December 2017 Investigation and fallout: Uber fires Sullivan; FTC investigation intensifies. Uber/FTC Sullivan charged with obstruction; $148M fine in 2018. |
September 2019 Legal resolution: Glover and Mereacre plead guilty to hacking charges. U.S. DOJ Sentenced to time served; restitution ordered. |
December 2022 Sullivan convicted: Guilty of obstruction and misprision of felony for concealing breach. U.S. DOJ Sentenced to 3 years probation; $100K fine. |

Dwell Time Analysis

Dwell time was approximately 1 month (October 24 to November 21, 2017, from detection to disclosure, but operational dwell was ~1 week from access to ransom), allowing data theft and negotiation undetected.

Step 1: Initial Access (October 2016) Step 2: Collection (October 24-28, 2016) Step 3: Ransom Demand (Late October 2016) Step 4: Payment/Cover-up (November 2016) Step 5: Detection/Disclosure (November 21, 2017)
Stolen GitHub credentials access AWS key.
What went wrong: No MFA on GitHub, leaked credentials.		 Downloaded 57M user records from S3 bucket.
Factor: Unencrypted data, no DLP.		 Contacted Sullivan, demanded $100K Bitcoin.
Factor: No incident response plan for ransomware.		 Paid ransom, signed NDAs to conceal.
Factor: FTC investigation ongoing.		 Public disclosure after internal review.
Total time: Exposed 57M records, led to legal fallout.

Dwell Time Insight: The dwell time was like a thief breaking in and looting undetected, enabled by poor credential hygiene. Median dwell time in 2016 was ~100 days (Mandiant); Uber’s short operational dwell was amplified by cover-up, leading to prolonged legal impact.
